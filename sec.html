English Conventions

These questions check whether a sentence follows the basic rules of written English — mostly punctuation and how the parts of a sentence fit together. The test hides which rule it's checking, so the real skill is learning to spot the rule, then applying it.

Start here: the two kinds of clause

Almost every punctuation question comes down to one idea: is each part of the sentence a complete thought or not?

Complete (independent)
Has a subject and a verb and could stand alone as a sentence.
The team won the game.
Incomplete (dependent)
Has a subject and verb but can't stand alone. Often starts with a joining word.
Because the team won the game…

The test: read the part by itself and ask, "Could this be its own sentence?" Yes = complete. No = incomplete.

Joining words that make a part incomplete: becausealthoughwhenifsincewhileafterthatwhich
Joining sentences (Boundaries)

A guide for choosing punctuation

Walk down this list. The first "yes" tells you what to use.

  1. Are both parts complete sentences? Use a period, a semicolon, or a comma + a joining word (and, but, or, so, yet, for, nor).
  2. Does the second part explain or introduce the first (and the first is complete)? Use a colon.
  3. Is it extra information you could remove and still have a sentence? Surround it with a pair of commas (or dashes).
  4. Does an incomplete part come first? Put a comma after it.
  5. None of these? Often no punctuation is needed.
The hike was long. It was worth it.  →  two complete sentences, so a period works. A comma alone would be wrong (that's a run-on).
Matching the parts (Form, Structure, and Sense)

Subject and verb agree

One thing takes a singular verb; more than one takes a plural verb. Watch for words that sneak between them.

The box of old photos was heavy. (The subject is "box," not "photos.")

Check: find the real subject, ignore the words in between, then match the verb.

Keep verb tense consistent

Don't switch between past and present without a reason.

She opened the door and walked in. (Both past — not "opened … walks.")

Check: what tense did the sentence start in? Stay there unless the meaning changes.

Pronouns are clear and agree

A pronoun (it, they, this) must clearly point to one noun and match it in number.

Each student brought their own lunch. (If "student" is singular, prefer "his or her" — or rewrite as "Students brought their…")

Check: ask "what exactly does this pronoun stand for?" If it's unclear or doesn't match, fix it.

You don't need to memorize rule names. For Boundaries, decide if each part is complete and walk the guide. For the rest, find the real subject, keep tense steady, and make every pronoun point clearly. Spotting which rule is being tested is the whole skill.
