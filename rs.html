Rhetorical Synthesis

These give you a short list of notes (facts about a topic) and a writing goal. Your job is to pick the sentence that uses the notes to do exactly what the goal asks. There's one habit that makes these much easier.

Read the goal first

Before you read the notes or the answers, read the goal sentence and be clear on what it asks for. Almost every wrong answer is a true sentence that does the wrong job.

  1. Read the goal and put it in your own words: what is this sentence supposed to do?
  2. Read the notes so you know the facts you have to work with.
  3. Pick the choice that does exactly that goal. Cross out any choice that is accurate but doesn't do the job.

Match the goal to the move

Goals usually ask for one of a few things. Spot the goal word, then do that move:

"emphasize a difference" → pick the sentence that puts two things side by side and contrasts them.
"introduce the topic to a new reader" → pick the broad, plain sentence, not a tiny detail.
"support a point with a specific detail" → pick the sentence with the exact fact or number.

The trap to watch for

Accurate but off-task. A choice can use the notes perfectly and still be wrong because it doesn't do what the goal asked. Being true isn't enough — it has to do the job.

So always check the choice against the goal, not just against the notes.

Read the goal first, say what it asks in your own words, then pick the choice that does exactly that. The facts in the notes are just your materials — the goal tells you what to build.
