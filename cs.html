Craft & Structure

These questions are about how a text is built and what its words mean. Three skills live here. Each has a simple move you can use on any passage.

1 · Word meaning (Words in Context)

The fastest move: cover the word, predict your own plain word from the sentence, then pick the choice closest to your prediction — checking it fits the tone.

This skill has its own full guide, including word roots that help with words you've never seen.

2 · What the author is doing (Text Structure and Purpose)

Don't ask what the text is about — ask what the author is doing. Name the action with a verb.

describesexplainsarguescomparestells a storygives an examplesolves a problem
  1. Read the whole text and say, in your own words, what the author is doing.
  2. For purpose, ask why the text exists — its main point. For structure, ask how it's ordered (a problem then a solution, a claim then evidence, general then specific).
  3. Pick the choice that matches the action — not just one that repeats a topic word.
A paragraph that names a problem and then offers a fix has the structure problem → solution, and its purpose is usually to propose that fix.

3 · Comparing two texts (Cross-Text Connections)

You get two short texts and a question about how they relate. The trick is to handle them one at a time first.

  1. Sum up Text 1 in one sentence: what does its author think?
  2. Do the same for Text 2.
  3. Name the relationship between the two authors.
agreedisagreeone adds to the otherone gives an exampleone qualifies the other
If Text 1 makes a claim and Text 2 shows a case where it doesn't hold, the second author disagrees with or limits the first.

One move per skill: predict the word from context, name what the author is doing, and summarize each text before comparing. Practice the move and it works on passages you've never seen.
