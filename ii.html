Information & Ideas

These questions ask you to find the main point, use evidence, and draw careful conclusions. Four skills, each with a clear move.

1 · The main idea (Central Ideas and Details)

Ask what the whole text is mostly about — not one sentence in it. The right answer covers the big point.

Reject choices that are: too narrow (just one detail), too broad (bigger than the text), or twisted (says something the text doesn't).

If a paragraph gives three reasons a city built more parks, the main idea is "why the city added parks" — not just the one reason mentioned last.

2 · Evidence from a text (Command of Evidence — Textual)

You're given a claim and asked which quotation or finding best supports it. Pick the one that supports the whole claim, directly.

A choice can be true and still be wrong if it only supports part of the claim, or is about something slightly different.

Claim: a method made roasting faster. The right evidence mentions time saved — not how the food tasted, even if that's also in the text.

3 · Evidence from data (Command of Evidence — Quantitative)

The passage comes with a table or graph. Slow down and read it before the choices.

Find the exact row or bar the sentence is about, read the axis or column label, and match the number to what the sentence claims. The right answer fits both the data and the point being made.

If the sentence says one group was highest, find the tallest bar (or biggest number) and name that group — don't trust memory, read it off the figure.

4 · Careful conclusions (Inferences)

An inference is a conclusion the text strongly points to but doesn't state outright. Choose the one the text supports, and no more.

Avoid choices that are too strong (always, never, proves) or that add ideas the text never gives. The safe answer stays close to the text.

If a text says a plan "may help" and "showed promise," a safe conclusion is "it could be useful" — not "it will definitely work."

Cover the whole point for main idea, match evidence to the full claim, read the figure before answering, and keep inferences close to what the text actually says. Same moves work on any passage.
