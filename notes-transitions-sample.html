Transitions

A transition is a word or phrase that shows how one sentence connects to the next. Your job is to find the connection first, then pick the word that fits it.

The habit that works every time

  1. Read both sentences fully. Make sure you know what each one is saying.
  2. Ask yourself: how does the second sentence relate to the first? Name it in your own words before you look at the choices.
  3. Pick the transition that matches the connection you named. Cross out any that point the wrong way.

Tip: decide the connection before reading the answer choices. The wrong choices are designed to look tempting if you skip that step.

The five connections · 0 of 5

1 · Adding more (Addition)

The second sentence adds another point that agrees with the first.

The museum is free on Sundays. In addition, students get in free any day.

Go-to words: also, in addition, furthermore, moreover

2 · Showing a difference (Contrast)

The second sentence goes against the first, or shows the other side.

The hike looked easy on the map. However, the trail was steep and rocky.

Go-to words: however, but, on the other hand, even so

3 · Cause and effect (Cause and Effect)

The first sentence makes the second one happen. One causes the other.

It rained all week. As a result, the game was moved to Saturday.

Go-to words: so, therefore, as a result, because of this

4 · Giving an example (Illustration)

The second sentence shows an example of what the first one said.

She loves outdoor sports. For example, she runs, swims, and climbs.

Go-to words: for example, for instance, such as

5 · Putting things in order (Sequence)

The sentences show steps or a sequence in time.

First, mix the dry ingredients. Next, add the eggs and milk.

Go-to words: first, next, then, finally, meanwhile

Try one. Which connection is this?
"The recipe calls for two cups of flour. ______, you can use one cup if you want a smaller batch."

This is a difference — the second sentence changes the first. A word like However fits. Watch out for "In addition," which would wrongly signal that you are adding more flour.

Take these one at a time. Once a connection feels automatic, mark it understood and move on. You don't need to memorize every word — you need to recognize the connection.
